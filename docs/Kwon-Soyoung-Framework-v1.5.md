Kwon Soyoung Interpretation & System Design Framework
Version 1.5
Subtitle
From Questions to Systems
A Framework for Interpreting Information Environments, Designing Participation, and Creating Public Meaning in the AI Era

Preface
This framework did not emerge from theory.

It emerged from practice.

It was developed through the analysis of 1,540 real questions collected from global information environments and through repeated attempts to understand what those questions reveal about Korea, public diplomacy, civic participation, AI, and society itself.

The framework began as a way to interpret questions.

It evolved into a way to design systems around them.

Its purpose is not merely to understand information environments.

Its purpose is to create new forms of participation, knowledge production, and collective meaning-making from them.

1. The Fundamental Premise
Most institutions optimize answers.

This framework optimizes questions.

Traditional information systems assume:


Question
↓
Answer
This framework assumes:


Question
↓
Interpretation
↓
Participation
↓
Knowledge
↓
New Questions
Questions are not deficiencies.

Questions are assets.

They reveal:

knowledge gaps

perception gaps

unmet needs

hidden assumptions

emerging narratives

future opportunities

Therefore:

A question should never be answered immediately.

A question should first be interpreted.

2. The Unit of Analysis
Most research analyzes:

content

media

messages

institutions

This framework analyzes:

curiosity.

The unit of analysis is not information.

The unit of analysis is the question that exists before information.

Because questions reveal what information environments have failed to provide.

3. The Five-Layer Model
All interpretation begins from the same movement.


Question
↓
Concept
↓
Theme
↓
Narrative
↓
Perception
Question
What people explicitly ask.

Example:

Why is Korea divided?

Concept
The issue hidden behind the question.

Example:

Division

Theme
The larger domain that contains the concept.

Example:

Geopolitics

Narrative
The story that organizes multiple concepts.

Example:

Korea is defined by conflict.

Perception
The image ultimately formed.

Example:

Korea is primarily understood as a divided nation.

The purpose of analysis is not classification.

The purpose is understanding how perceptions emerge.

4. Core Interpretation Principles
Principle 1
Attention is not understanding.
Visibility does not equal comprehension.

Reach does not equal meaning.

A nation may become more visible while remaining poorly understood.

Therefore:

The key question is never:

How much attention did we receive?

The key question is:

What understanding did that attention produce?

Principle 2
Curiosity matters more than promotion.
Traditional communication begins with supply.

This framework begins with demand.

Wrong question:

What should Korea promote?

Right question:

What does the world already want to understand?

Questions are demand signals.

Promotion should follow curiosity.

Principle 3
Patterns are symptoms.
Structures are causes.
Observed patterns are rarely the real finding.

Example:


Many people ask:

"Is Korean difficult?"
Observation:

People think Korean is difficult.

Structural interpretation:

Korea has generated interest faster than accessibility.

The framework searches for structures, not patterns.

Principle 4
Every perception is systemic.
Perceptions are not isolated beliefs.

They are outputs of interconnected systems.

Example:


K-pop
↓
Language
↓
Culture
↓
Identity
↓
Country Image
The goal is to map relationships.

Not count mentions.

Principle 5
Every dataset contains institutional questions.
Questions are rarely only informational.

They often indicate missing structures.

Example:


Why are there two Koreas?
Informational question.

But also:


Who is responsible for helping people understand Korea?
Institutional question.

The framework seeks hidden institutional implications.

5. The Escalation Ladder
Analysis must continuously move upward.

Level 1
Observation


People ask about Korean language.
Level 2
Meaning


People are interested in Korea.
Level 3
Gap


Interest exceeds accessibility.
Level 4
Strategic Implication


Reducing barriers is more important than attracting attention.
Level 5
System Implication


A national onboarding ecosystem is missing.
No analysis should stop below Level 4.

The framework's purpose is escalation.

6. Question-to-System Principle
One of the defining principles of this framework.

Every persistent question implies a missing system.
Repeated questions are rarely evidence of individual ignorance.

They are evidence of structural absence.

Example:


Why is Korea divided?
appears repeatedly for years.

The finding is not:

People don't know.

The finding is:

Existing systems have failed to help people understand.

Questions are diagnostic tools.

They identify missing infrastructure.

7. Frame Competition Principle
National images are not collections of perceptions.

They are competitions between perceptions.

The key question is not:


What is visible?
The key question is:


Which frame is winning?
Example:


K-pop questions increase.
Interesting.

But the real question:


Has the K-pop frame replaced the division frame?
National image is the outcome of frame competition.

Not content accumulation.

12. Human–AI Boundary
The AI era makes this distinction essential.

AI and humans create value differently.

AI excels at:
collection

translation

clustering

summarization

visualization

pattern detection

Humans remain responsible for:
significance

interpretation

implication

judgment

meaning

system design

AI produces findings.

Humans produce significance.

AI can tell us what changed.

Humans decide why it matters.

13. System Design Layer
Interpretation is incomplete until it produces a new possibility.

This framework therefore extends beyond analysis.

Every significant finding should eventually generate one of three outputs:

Strategic Output
What should change?

Participation Output
Who should participate?

System Output
What structure should exist?

Example:


People ask about Korean language.
↓

Strategic:

Reduce onboarding barriers.

↓

Participation:

Teachers and diaspora create entry pathways.

↓

System:

A multilingual Korean-learning commons.

Analysis without design remains incomplete.

16. Final Thesis
Most societies treat questions as temporary requests.

This framework treats questions as social infrastructure.

Most systems attempt to answer questions.

This framework attempts to understand what questions reveal.

Most institutions optimize communication.

This framework optimizes comprehension.

Most AI systems generate answers.

This framework uses AI to expose the questions beneath them.

Because the future of public diplomacy, education, civic participation, and knowledge creation will not be determined by what institutions say.

It will be determined by how societies interpret the questions people continue to ask.
